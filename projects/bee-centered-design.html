The Disappearing Buzz

India is home to 719 bee species, but a lack of data and understanding threatens their conservation. How can we make urban dwellers, especially children, care about solitary bees?

Friend Or Foe

Our relationship with bees is often transactional and fearful. We see them as honey producers or stinging threats, failing to recognize them as keystone pollinators essential to our ecosystem.

Bee populations are in sharp decline due to pesticides, habitat loss, and invasive species. But in India, a critical problem is the lack of data. We don't even know what we're losing, making conservation efforts difficult.

Mapping our 'pain points' against a bee's revealed a profound misunderstanding. Our fear of a sting is temporary, but for a bee, stinging is often fatal. They are not aggressive; they are defensive.

The bee's decline affects everyone, from tribal honey hunters whose livelihoods are threatened to farmers and urban consumers. Conservation requires a unified effort across this entire ecosystem.

Using the 5 Whys and 1 How method, I drilled down to the core issue: a fundamental lack of awareness and empathy, especially in urban areas where bees are seen as a nuisance, not a necessity.

I also looked to India's tribal communities, like the Sholigas and Todas, who practice sustainable honey hunting. They understand the balance, but their knowledge and livelihoods are under threat from commercial practices and the very decline they seek to prevent.

My research led me to solitary bees, like the Leafcutter. They don't live in hives, make honey, or sting aggressively. Yet, they are pollination powerhouses, responsible for a significant amount of plant reproduction. This was the key insight.

The Product: A Taste of Discovery

An edible, interactive dessert inspired by the nest of the native Leafcutter bee. Green on the outside (leaves), yellow on the inside (pollen), it turns learning into a delicious, hands-on experience.

Nest-Inspired Packaging

Sustainable packaging inspired by Japanese bamboo design, mimicking the segmented structure of a leafcutter bee's nest to reinforce the product story.

Beyond Human-Centered Design

This project taught me that design isn't meant to be centered solely around humans. Bee Centered Design is a philosophy of creating with empathy for all organisms, questioning if our intervention is needed, and designing for the entire ecosystem we are a part of.
